Project Scope

Background Literature

The background literature highlights the integration of satellite imagery, remote sensing technologies, and computational methods in environmental monitoring, focusing on deforestation detection, forest classification, forest health assessments, and wildlife tracking.


Deforestation Detection:

Landsat 8 OLI and Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) imagery, along with deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs), enhance deforestation mapping and overcome challenges like cloud cover.


Forest Classification:

Modern remote sensing technologies like LiDAR have advanced the precise and scalable classification of forest types, aiding conservation and land management.


Forest Health Assessments:

LiDAR provides detailed biomass and structural data, with machine learning algorithms further refining forest health monitoring and biomass estimation.


Wildlife Tracking:

Remote camera traps and deep learning techniques (e.g., CNNs and YOLO algorithms) enable accurate, non-invasive wildlife monitoring and species identification.

    Proposed Problem

    Enhancing Precision and Automation in Environmental Monitoring: Addressing the Gaps in Deforestation Detection, Forest Classification, Forest Health Assessment, and Wildlife Tracking

    Current environmental monitoring practices face critical limitations in precision, automation, and comprehensiveness. Deforestation detection methods relying heavily on satellite imagery lack the precision for accurate classification. Forest classification is challenged by the underutilization of LiDAR data, making automated identification labor-intensive. Forest health assessments do not integrate multiple data sources, leading to fragmented analyses. Wildlife tracking systems struggle with inaccuracies, especially in low-light conditions, and lack real-time data processing and actionable insights. These gaps hinder effective environmental management and conservation efforts.


    Proposed Solution

    Develop a comprehensive, integrated environmental monitoring system leveraging advanced technologies such as LiDAR, SAR, multispectral satellite imagery, and machine learning algorithms to enhance the precision, automation, and comprehensiveness of deforestation detection, forest classification, forest health assessment, and wildlife tracking.

    Research Objectives

    Deforestation Detection and Classification

    The first objective is to enhance deforestation detection methods by integrating LiDAR technology with traditional satellite imagery to provide precise classification and quantification of deforestation. This includes distinguishing various causes of deforestation, such as agricultural expansion, new constructions, and urbanization, to improve the accuracy and depth of assessments. This approach aims to support effective forest management and conservation strategies by offering detailed and actionable insights.

    Automated Forest Type Classification

    The second objective is to develop an advanced system for the automated identification of forest types using LiDAR data combined with multispectral satellite imagery and climatic data. This involves creating machine learning models, including convolutional neural networks (CNNs), to process and analyze the data, enabling efficient and scalable forest type classification. The goal is to replace labor-intensive manual methods with a comprehensive, automated solution that provides accurate and consistent classification across diverse forest ecosystems.

    Comprehensive Forest Health Assessment

    The third objective is to create tools for assessing forest health by evaluating biomass estimation and carbon storage using LiDAR data. This includes developing methodologies to monitor changes in forest health over time, facilitating the identification of patterns in carbon storage, and supporting sustainable forest management practices. The aim is to fill the gap in regions like Sri Lanka by providing a robust system for tracking forest conditions and guiding conservation efforts and climate change mitigation strategies.

    Enhanced Wildlife Tracking and Monitoring

    The fourth objective is to improve wildlife tracking accuracy through the development of advanced detection models capable of identifying animals in challenging conditions, such as low-light environments and partial visibility. This includes refining techniques for counting animals within groups and integrating data from camera traps into a user-friendly application. The application will offer functionalities for low-light analysis, group counting, and seamless data integration, enabling more effective conservation strategies and real-time monitoring of wildlife populations.

    Methodology

    Figure 2. High Level Architecture of the system.

    Our methodology for improving environmental monitoring combines cutting edge technologies and techniques with four key elements: tracking wildlife, classifying forests, determining the health of the forests, and detecting deforestation. We will use deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs) tailored to process Landsat 8 OLI and Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) data for the purpose of detecting deforestation. This strategy seeks to improve accuracy by reducing obstacles like cloud cover and fluctuating climatic conditions. Our approach to forest classification is applying automated methods that make use of LiDAR data to accurately classify different types of forests. Creating scalable algorithms to automate land management and conservation procedures will help to decrease the need for human intervention.

    Moreover, our approach incorporates many data sources for an all-encompassing evaluation of forest health. We aim to properly assess biomass and carbon storage by utilizing cutting-edge image processing techniques and machine learning algorithms, with a special emphasis on LiDAR data. In addition to increasing the accuracy of assessments of the health of the forest, this comprehensive method offers practical insights that are essential for efficient environmental management and conservation initiatives. Lastly, by utilizing deep learning models like CNNs and YOLO algorithms, our methodology increases the monitoring of wildlife. In order to assist effective conservation measures, these models will be tuned for real-time monitoring through the use of remote video traps. This will address issues with low light and improve the accuracy of species identification.
